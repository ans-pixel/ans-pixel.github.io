 <p>
        The mammalian brain has three fundamental parts: the cerebrum, the cerebellum, and the brain stem (consisting of the pons and the medulla). These three regions are functionally distinct, with the cerebrum being responsible for higher cognitive function and is also the seat for sensory processing. The cerebellum is the brain structure associated with coordinating motor functions and maintaining balance. The brainstem region hosts the medulla which is responsible for performing numerous involuntary functions such as respiration.

 <summary><span class = "accordion_header" style = "display: inline;">The Spinal Cord</span></summary>
 <div class = "content_container">
 <p>
        The spinal cord is continuous with the brain stem and the medulla oblongata and extends till the first lumbar vertebra. The terminal region of the spinal cord, at its base is marked by the cauda equina (Latin for horse tail), which is a collection of the lumbar, sacral and coccygeal nerve fibres. The spinal cord gives rise to 31 pairs of spinal nerves: 8 pairs arising from the cervical vertebrae, 12 pairs arising from the thoracic vertebrae, 5 pairs arising from the sacral vertebrae, 5 pairs arising at the sacral level and 1 pair arising at the coccygeal level. Together, these nerves serve to control the brain with the rest of the nervous system and the spinal cord acts as the buffer between the central and peripheral nervous system.
        <br>
        <br>
        The spinal cord has an architecture opposite that of the brain, with the white matter on the outside and the grey matter on the inside. This is evident in sections of the spinal cord where the neuronal somas may be observed in the central region. The spinal cord is also hollow along the middle, with the cavity being continuous with the fourth ventricle of the brain and containing cerebrospinal fluid. In addition, the spinal cord is also the seat of certain reflex arcs, considering that all sensory information must first pass through the spinal cord to reach the brain. In case of scenarios where a reflex action is required, the sensory neuron synapses directly on to the motor neuron, as in the case of the neuronal arrangement of the knee-jerk reflex, with the sensory neuron synapsing directly on to the motor neuron for the extensor muscle. This sensory neuron also synapses on to an interneuron which synapses on to the motor neuron for the flexor muscle, and inhibits it, leading to the net extension of the leg in the knee jerk reflex.
    </p>
